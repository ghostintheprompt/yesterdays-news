# Yesterday's News

On the desk, one of the meaner jokes was telling a guy on the phone he was reading yesterday's news.

If he was a tourist, he took it personally.

If he was real, he understood the insult immediately.

It did not mean the story was false. It meant the story had already been eaten.

That is the first thing people outside markets miss. Being right is not enough. Being early is not the same as being smart, but the market is often rude enough to make the two feel indistinguishable for long stretches. By the time the explanation looks clean, by the time the note is polished, by the time the conviction arrives dressed like certainty, price may already be smoking in the parking lot with your edge in its pocket.

That is what the joke was really about.

The phone guys would pitch some stock like it had just descended from Sinai. Then somebody on the desk would say, “That’s yesterday’s news,” and wait to see what kind of man they had on the line. If he had any pulse, he knew the problem. Maybe the thesis was still alive. Maybe the company was still good. Maybe the move even had more room in it. But if the catalyst had already gone through the tape, if the street had already metabolized the surprise, then what exactly were we being sold besides delayed conviction and a well-dressed obituary.

That distinction matters more now, not less.

There is more market commentary than ever, and most of it arrives with the same old disease: narrative freshness disguised as tradable freshness. Somebody sees a move, waits for the pattern to become legible, pulls together the reasons, adds a little authority, and hands you the explanation after the dangerous part is over. The story is coherent because the uncertainty has already been killed off. What you are buying at that point is not edge. It is emotional closure.

That is why I wanted to build *Yesterday's News*.

Not as a magic signal machine. Not as a fake alpha cathedral. Not as one more dashboard trying to pretend it knows the future better than the market does. More as a rude little instrument for asking the right first question: is this still alive, or am I being handed a corpse with a chart attached.

That question can be formalized.

How long after the event did the article hit. How much of the move happened before publication. Whether volume came in before the note or after it. Whether the piece contains genuinely new information or just a cleaner arrangement of what the tape has already priced. The useful thing is not pretending those questions produce a perfect answer. The useful thing is forcing them to exist before the conviction speech starts.

That is the spirit of the thing.

There are too many people in markets who think understanding what happened is the same as having had a shot at the trade. It is not. Plenty of analysis is smart, and late. Plenty of commentary is accurate, and dead. Plenty of finance media exists mostly to explain the move after the move has already made the only decision that mattered.

Yesterday’s news.

Still true. Still funny. Still expensive if you miss what it means.
